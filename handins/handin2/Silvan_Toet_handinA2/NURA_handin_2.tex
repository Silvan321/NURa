\section{Satellite galaxies around a massive central}

\subsection{(1a) Determining the normalization}
The spherical volume element \(dV\) can be defined as follows:
We know that the volume can be computed in spherical coordinates using the triple integral
\begin{align}
  \iiint_V n(x) dV & = \int_{\phi=0}^{2\pi} \int_{\theta=0}^{\pi} \int_{r=0}^{r_{max}} n(x) r^2 \sin\theta \, dr\,d\theta\,d\phi                                                \\
                   & = 4 \pi A \langle N_{sat} \rangle \int_{x=0}^{x_{max}=5} x^2 \left(\frac{x}{b}\right)^{a-3} \exp(-\left(\frac{x}{b}\right)^c) dx = \langle N_{sat} \rangle \\
  -> A             & = \frac{1}{4 \pi \int_{x=0}^{x_{max}=5} x^2 \left(\frac{x}{b}\right)^{a-3} \exp(-\left(\frac{x}{b}\right)^c) dx}
\end{align}
Since n(x) where x is the radius relative to virial radius \(x=r/r_{vir}\) is only dependent on r, we can easily solve for \(\theta\) and \(\phi\).
Dividing by \(\langle N_{sat} \rangle\) on both sides and rearranging gives us an expression for \(A\).

Next we use Romberg integration (i.e. Richardson extrapolation applied to integration) to numerically integrate the integral in the expression.
Romberg integration first applies the extended trapezoid rule for N intervals, then 2N, 4N, ... up to $2^m\times$ N intervals, where m is the order of the initial approximations.
Next we use the fact that each successive order's leading error term is exactly a 1/4 times the leading error term of the previous order.
Subtracting the two approximations from each other in such a way as to cancel the leading error term results in m-1 new approximations.
We can do this subtraction trick m-1 times. Since after each subtraction we no longer need the two values used for this, we can store the initial approximations and all subsequent improvements in a vector of length m.
At the end of the algorithm the first element of the vector contains the best estimate of the integral and the best estimate for the error is the absolute difference between the first two elements.
Table~\ref{table_A_constant} shows the value of the normalization constant \(A\) to full precision.

\subsection{(1b) Sampling the satellite distribution}
We use rejection sampling to generate 10000 satellite galaxy positions. In rejection sampling we first scale the probability distribution function max value to 1.
Then we generate two random numbers:
\begin{itemize}
  \item one random number between the x ranges of our pdf. In our case this is between \(x_{min}\)=0 and \(x_{max}\)=5
  \item another random number between 0 and 1. This number represents the probability that the number is accepted
\end{itemize}
if y $<$ p(x), we accept the generated x value in our sample. Since y is more likely to lie under p(x) if p(x) is large for that value of x, this incorporates the difference between probabilities for different x values.
Hence the statement "2 random numbers in, $<$1 random number out", because we need two random numbers to generate at most 1 (we might also reject it).

We implement our random number generator as a combination of an 64-bit XOR shift method and a (Multiple) Linear Congruential Generator.
Both sub RNGs receive their own starting seed, and base their subsequent generated number on only (a part of) their own previous output.
The two random numbers generated by the subgenerators are then added element-wise in the combination-rng.

\subsection{(1c) Selecting and sorting satellites}
We are asked to select 100 galaxies with equal probability, without duplicates and without rejection of a generated sample.
As explained during the lecture, a Linear Congruential Generator is not a great RNG on its own, partially due to the fact it cannot generate duplicate samples before it starts to repeat itself.
Once it starts to repeat itself, the entire sequence repeats (becomes periodic).
But this means that if we choose our LCG parameters (a,c and m) right, we can ensure that we get 100 random numbers with equal probability without a duplicate!
A quick search on LCGs on wikipedia (\url{https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_congruential_generator}) reveals that if we satisfy the Hull-Dobell theorem, we are guaranteed to get a full sequence of period m for every seed value x.
The three criteria to be met are:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item m and c are comprime
  \item a - 1 is divisible by all prime factors of m
  \item a - 1 is divisible by 4 if m is divisible by 4
\end{enumerate}
We choose m to be the size of the array that we sample from, in our case 10000 which is the number of initally generated satellite galaxies.
Next we choose a and c to satisfy the above criteria. m has as prime factors 2 and 5. So choose c as odd and not divisible by 5.
a must be divisible by 2, 4 and 5. We choose a=21 and c=37. The generated indices binned are shown in figure \ref{choice_test}.

Now we sort the 100 selected galaxies using our sorting algorithm.
Our first attempt was to use our quicksort algorithm.
The quicksort algorithm avoids the pitfall of simple sorting techniques such as selection sort that need N$^2$ comparisons for an array of length N.
It does this by splitting the array into smaller and smaller subarrays and sorting these recursively.
We first use selection sort to choose the pivot element from the first, middle and last element.
Then we start swapping elements to the left of the pivot that are larger than the pivot with elements to the right of the pivot that are smaller than the pivot.
This ensures that after 1 recursion, we end up with two subarrays that, although they are still unsorted themselves, their elements are in the correct position compared to the pivot element.
Doing this recursively eventually sorts the entire array with an order O(N log N) comparisons.

Unfortunately, checking the array after the quicksort call revealed there were some values not sorted correctly.
We therefore resort to our own selection sort as a fallback option for this assignment.
We have left both algorithms in the code for inspection and will be very happy if any of the graders can spot the mistake in the quicksort algorithm.

Now we compare the analytical derivative of n(x), namely dn(x)/dx, to the numerical derivative.
Our plan was to use Ridder's method to accurately compute the numerical derivative.
Ridder's method applies Richardson extrapolation to differentiation.
We first generate m approximations with decreasing step sizes of h using the central difference method.
Then we use the update rule
\begin{equation}
  D_{i,j} = \frac{d^{2j} D_{i+1, j-1} - D_{i,j-1}}{d^{2j}-1}
\end{equation}
To cancel out error terms shared by the approximations.
This builds a piramide, or more efficiently, a vector, where the first element holds the best approximation of the derivative.

Unfortunately we could not get Ridder's method to work for the numerical derivative.
We therefore use the central difference method.

\subsection{(2a) Photoionization heating and radiative recombination cooling}
root finding deals with finding the point x on the abscissa where a function f(x) crosses it e.g. f(x) = 0.
This can also be used to find intersections (in this case the equilibrium temperature) of two functions, simply by defining the difference function h(x) = g(x) - f(x).
We decided to compare three different root finding methods: bisection, secant and false position.
In all three methods we start with a function and a bracket [a,b] in which a single root must lie.

This can be determined by checking if f(a) * f(b) < 0: one function value must be positive and one negative and therefore their product negative.
Bisection takes a new point c halfway between a and b and evaluates the function there. If the root is between a and c, this becomes the new interval. Same for b and c.
a,b and c are updated in subsequent steps.

Secant determines a new point c by drawing a line from a to b and looking where it hits the x axis.
again, a,b and c are updated in subsequent steps.
However, this method is prone to divergence: the new point c could land outside the original bracket.

False position does essentially the same as secant, but also keeps the counterpoint (the previous best estimate).
It then checks if the new point would fall outside the bracket, and if so, use the counterpoint.

Secant and false position can in theory converge faster than bisection, but can get stuck on function that suddenly start to grow/shrink rapidly.
It is always a good idea to first examine our function.

\section{Conclusions}
This is short summary as I have moved the main discussion of the results to each subquestion.
In the first exercise we first integrated a satellite galaxy distribution using Romberg integration.
We then generated 10000 satellites from the probability distribution using rejection sampling and our own combined RNG, made up of an 64 bit XOR shift and Linear Congruential Generator.
We compared the generated values to the analytical distribution.
Next we randomly selected 100 galaxies without duplicates or rejection using an LCG with a full period using the Hull-Dobell theorem.
Then we sorted these: first we tried our implementation of quicksort. When this failed we resorted to our implementation of heapsort.
Finally we took the numerical derivative of the distribution and compared this to the analytical distribution.
We first tried Ridder's method for derivation. When this failed we resorted to central differences.

In the second exercise we used three different implementations of root finding algorithms, namely bisection, secant and false position, to find the roots of two equilibrium temperature functions of HII regions.
Due the rapidly decreasing nature of the functions, bisection performed the best of the three algorithms.
